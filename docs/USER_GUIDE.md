# CVI Risk Predictor: User Guide

**For Community Violence Intervention (CVI) Practitioners**

**Last Updated:** January 23, 2026

---

## 1. Welcome to the CVI Risk Predictor

Thank you for your life-saving work in the community. This tool is designed to support you by providing data-driven insights to help you anticipate where and when your skills are needed most. It is a decision-support tool, meant to complement your invaluable experience and knowledge of the communities you serve.

**Remember:** This tool predicts risk for a **neighborhood**, not a person. It is a smoke alarm, not a crystal ball.

---

## 2. Getting Started: The Dashboard

The main dashboard gives you a high-level overview of violence risk in your city.

### 2.1. Selecting Your City

Use the dropdown menu at the top of the page to select the city you are working in. The map and all statistics will automatically update.

### 2.2. Understanding the Main Statistics

At the top of the dashboard, you will see four key numbers:

-   **Total Neighborhoods:** The total number of neighborhoods or community areas defined for the selected city.
-   **Critical Risk:** The number of neighborhoods currently classified in the highest risk tier. These are areas that may require immediate attention.
-   **High Risk:** The number of neighborhoods in the second-highest risk tier. These areas are experiencing elevated levels of risk.
-   **Low Risk:** The number of neighborhoods with the lowest levels of violence risk.

### 2.3. The Risk Map

The central feature of the dashboard is the interactive risk map. Here’s how to use it:

-   **Colors:** Each neighborhood is color-coded based on its predicted risk level for the upcoming week.
    -   <span style="color: #e74c3c;">**Red (Critical):**</span> Highest risk. Requires immediate strategic attention.
    -   <span style="color: #e67e22;">**Orange (High):**</span> Elevated risk. Proactive outreach is recommended.
    -   <span style="color: #f39c12;">**Yellow (Moderate):**</span> Moderate risk. Monitor for changing conditions.
    -   <span style="color: #2ecc71;">**Green (Low):**</span> Lowest risk.
-   **Clicking on a Neighborhood:** Click on any neighborhood on the map to bring up a detailed information panel.

## 3. Deep Dive: The Neighborhood Information Panel

When you click on a neighborhood, a panel appears with detailed information to help you understand the local context.

### 3.1. Key Information

-   **Neighborhood Name:** The name of the selected area.
-   **Risk Level & Score:** The predicted risk level (e.g., 
`High`) and the underlying numerical score (0-100).

### 3.2. Key Risk Drivers

This section helps you understand *why* a neighborhood is at a heightened risk. It lists the top 3-5 factors that the model identified as contributing most to the risk score. Examples might include:

-   `Recent increase in violent crime`
-   `High density of abandoned buildings`
-   `High social vulnerability score`
-   `Spike in ShotSpotter alerts`

Use these drivers to tailor your intervention strategy. For example, if abandoned buildings are a key factor, this might suggest opportunities for community clean-up events or advocating with the city for remediation.

### 3.3. Historical Trend

A simple line chart shows the number of violent incidents in that neighborhood over the past several weeks. This helps you see:

-   If the current risk is part of a sustained trend or a sudden spike.
-   The general baseline of violence in the area.

### 3.4. Strategic Recommendations

Based on the risk level and key drivers, the tool provides a list of potential strategic recommendations. These are suggestions to be considered alongside your own professional judgment.

**Example Recommendations:**

> -   **For a neighborhood with rising crime trend:** *"Increase street outreach presence during evening and weekend hours."*
> -   **For a neighborhood with high social vulnerability:** *"Partner with local service providers to host a resource fair."*
> -   **For a neighborhood with a spike in ShotSpotter alerts:** *"Focus on conflict mediation and rumor control to prevent retaliatory violence."*

## 4. How to Use This Information

This tool is most powerful when integrated into your regular workflow.

### 4.1. Weekly Team Meetings

-   Review the citywide risk map at the start of your weekly planning meeting.
-   Identify the top 3-5 neighborhoods in the "Critical" and "High" risk categories.
-   Discuss the key risk drivers for these neighborhoods. Does this match what your team is seeing on the ground?
-   Use the information to decide where to focus your team's efforts for the upcoming week.

### 4.2. Strategic Planning

-   Use the long-term risk tier classifications (from the clustering model) to develop strategic plans for the next 3-6 months.
-   Identify areas that are persistently high-risk and may require sustained investment or new partnerships.

### 4.3. Reporting and Grant Writing

-   Export data and charts from the tool to include in reports to funders.
-   Use the objective, data-driven risk assessments to build a strong case for additional resources in your grant applications.

---

## 5. Important Reminders

-   **You are the expert.** This tool is a source of information, not a source of orders. Your knowledge of the community, your relationships, and your experience are more valuable than any algorithm.
-   **Context is key.** The data doesn't capture everything. Always consider the local context that you know from being on the ground.
-   **Data has biases.** Remember that crime data reflects what is reported. Use this tool as one piece of the puzzle.
